Maraga Succession: Herman Manyora Claims Interviews for CJ Position Are Sub-standard

Posted by Hettie Henneman on Monday, May 27, 2024

- Herman Manyora said the whole exercise had fallen short of professionalism

- The political analyst expressed his dissatisfaction with the way the exercise was being conducted

- According to him, the questions posed to the interviewees did not befit a potential holder of the chief justice's office

Political commentator Herman Manyora has poked holes in the exercise of vetting applicants seeking to occupy the chief justice office after the exit of David Maraga.

Today, Wednesday, April 14, being the third day since the commencement of the interviews, Manyora said the happenings during the exercise did not befit the search for the next CJ.

Manyora claimed the vetting, which was being overseen by the Judicial Service Commission (JSC), was marred with unnecessary flaws that did not reflect the stature of the office.

"Somebody please stop this Judiciary drama from playing on our TVs. The whole interview appears to be not for a CJ but perhaps for a junior magistrate," tweeted Manyora.

According to Manyora, the way the players in the whole exercise carried themselves was low-grade, notwithstanding the questions posed.

"Everything is an embarrassment to the country. Not the interviewees; not the interviewers not the questions," he said.

Manyora's sentiments were echoed by LSK president Nelson Havi who said the vetting panel was not as animated as had been in previous interviews for the same post.

"We miss Ahmednasir Abdullahi SC and Prof Tom Ojienda SC on that significant but currently “idle and empty” seat at the Judicial Service Commission," Havi said.

The interviews began on Monday, April 12, and on a dramatic note after Justice Said Chitembwe broke down while submitting to the panel.

During the interview, Chitembwe was asked about a ruling he made in a corruption case in 2009, an experienced he recalled with high emotions.

The judge, while defending his decision then, said it was important for judges to objectively sift through any cases brought before them to determine the ones laced with malice from authentic ones.

"If an accused comes before you and raises his hand, hear him," Chitembwe said while struggling to contain his tears.

He also defended his past ruling on a defilement case in which he set free a man serving a 20-year jail term for defiling a 13-year-old girl.

"It is not that I am advocating for the reduction of the age of consent. The issue is not even the age of consent but the social aspect of the law. Is the law serving the people, and how do we interpret it?" posed Chitembwe.

The vetting exercise will end on April 23.

Others eyeing the position are Patricia Mbote, Lady Justice Martha Koome, Justice Marete Njagi, Philip Murgor, Justice Matthews Nduma, Lawyer Fredrick Ngatia, Justice William Ouko (Court of Appeal president), Wekesa Moni, and Ms Alice Y.
