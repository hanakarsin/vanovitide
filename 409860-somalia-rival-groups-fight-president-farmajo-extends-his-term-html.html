Somalia: Rival Groups Fight after President Farmajo Extends His Term

Posted by Lourie Helzer on Saturday, June 29, 2024

- On Monday, April 13, Farmajo signed a law that would see him extend his four-year term in office for the next two years

- The country's media outlets reported that the fight was between pro-government forces and military units that support the opposition

- Farmajo’s four-year term expired in February 2021, without a successor

Somalia’s president Mohamed Abdullahi Farmajo's supporters clashed with those opposed to his move to extend his term at the streets of the country's capital, Mogadishu.

On Monday, April 13, Farmajo, the chairman of the Tayo party, signed a law that would extend his four-year term in office for the next two years.

Details as of the Sunday, April 25, violence were sketchy, but the country's media outlets reported that there was heavy gunfire in the North of Mogadishu.

Morad news website tweeted that the fight was between pro-government forces and military units that support the opposition.

According to BBC, the anti-government protestors were heard chanting, "We do not want a term extension. We do not want Farmajo. We do not want dictatorship."

Farmajo’s four-year term expired in February 2021, without a successor.

There have been threats of sanctions from the international community over Farmaajo's recent actions.

As previously reported, a new group of legislators was expected to pick a new president, but they saw their own selection delayed after opponents accused the head of state of packing regional and national elections boards with his own supporters.

The lower house of Somalia’s parliament voted to extend Farmajo’s term in the volatile nation, but the move was dealt a blow after the House’s upper chamber swiftly rejected it.

The United States, Somalia’s main donor, maintained that it would not support any term extension.

“We have stressed repeatedly that it is vital for the peace, stability, prosperity, and governance of Somalia that the Federal Government and the Federal Member States reach a consensus on a way forward for the electoral process,” said U.S. Secretary of State Antony J. Blinken.

The Joe Biden led-administration further said that the US does not support parallel or partial electoral processes.
