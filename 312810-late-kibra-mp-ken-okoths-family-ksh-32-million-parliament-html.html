Late Kibra MP Ken Okoth's family to get KSh 32 million from Parliament

Posted by Hettie Henneman on Friday, July 26, 2024

- The money is part of the pension that the family is entitled to, given he served two terms as MP

- Family is yet to settle on the administrator of the late MP's estate

- Parliament will wait until it is given a go ahead after the court settles on an administrator

The family of the late Kibra MP Ken Okoth is set to receive KSh 32 million from Parliament which includes his group life insurance cover and death gratuity.

However, the family will have to wait a little bit longer before it receives the money as Parliament has to receive a letter from the court indicating who the administrator of the late lawmaker's estate is.

According to the Daily Nation, once the family agrees on the estate administrator, the name should be publicised in a gazette notice so that anybody with an issue can raise it..

“Yes, the family is entitled to KSh 32 million before tax, but we are not releasing the money now. We have to follow the law, which requires that we have to first get the letter of the administrator of the estate,” said a source who deals with MPs’ pensions.

Asked on whether the family had settled on the estate administrator, Okoth's brother Imran said the matter was a private affair and lawyers was handling the issue.

“I don’t know why they are telling you the amount and all that. Why would they do that yet these are personal matters?” he posed.

Normally, MPs contribute 12.5% of their salary as pension and all the emoluments includes salary, responsibility allowance, constituency allowance, nominated member’s allowance, ex officio member’s allowance, house allowance, accommodation allowance and sitting allowance.

The Parliamentary Pensions Act stipulates that only those who serve for two terms or more are entitled to a monthly pension of at least KSh 125,000 for the rest of their lives.

Those who serve for less than two terms are entitled to get a refund of their contributions and a 15% annual interest.

Recently, National Assembly Minority Leader John Mbadi proposed amendments to the act which seeks to have MPs who retired between 1984 and 2001 paid KSh 100,000 per month for life.
