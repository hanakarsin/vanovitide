My Ex-Husband Stole over KSh 700k of Our Sons Education Money, Leila Buraje

Posted by Hettie Henneman on Friday, April 26, 2024
  • Leila took out education policies for her two sons to secure their future
  • She added her then-husband as a policyholder of the older son’s policy before the separation
  • The payments were to be disbursed in partial maturities of KSh 69000 yearly, starting 2018 to 2022
  • Upon maturity, the ex-husband then withdrew all the money just before the son joined high-school
  • The ex-husband also took a loan against the second son’s policy and has never repaid

When an insurance agent approached Leila Buraje with the idea of saving for her sons’ education, the firstborn son was only six years old.

“This was such a noble idea and after doing some research I loved the idea because it meant securing my child’s education ahead of time,” Leila said.

Being in the informal sector where a salary was not guaranteed or predictable, she bought this idea to avoid struggling for high school as well as university school fees.

“I never went to the university but I really wanted my children to have what I missed to avoid a repeat of history,” she said.

Leila quickly signed the documents and the agent mentioned that she should also consider involving her husband as a policyholder on the same education cover as well. The idea was for the two parents to contribute towards their kid’s education fund.

“She told me that I needed to do that so that just in case anything happened, the kids would still be covered,” Leila recalled, adding that this looked harmless to her.“After all this was for our common interest, which was our children. So I carried the documents home, explained to Mr. Man (her husband) that I had decided to take academic policies for the boys and that I wanted him to be a holder on one.” “He was okay with being a holder, but quickly distanced himself from paying the premiums, he said that since this was my idea, he was not going to commit.” She adds.

So the husband signed to be the policyholder of the older boy but did not agree to contribute towards the kitty. This left Leila to source for funds to remit into the policies as per her agreement with the insurance.

“Sometimes I could go several months without paying but when I did I would cover all the arrears and keep going. At some point the policies almost lapsed due to the huge arrears, I walked to the office and my agent advised me to concentrate on the older boy's policy since he was in upper primary,” she recalled.

All this time, the husband never bothered to find out how the policies were running and if she needed any help.

“This however did not bother me as I had decided to secure the future of our children,” she told TUKO.co.ke.

Things went well until a time when she and her husband could no longer be together as the relationship got seriously strained.

“The inevitable happened and we parted ways,” she said.

Remembering she had included her now ex-husband as a policyholder, she went to her insurance agency to find out if that would affect her sons’ covers. She had been contributing monthly premiums of KSh 5130 for each son’s policy.

“I was told not to worry but just continue paying, that when the policy gets to partial maturity, they will simply do a cheque to school. I was told that it was a bit late to change it back to my name because it had paid for many years and was almost getting to partial maturity.”

This comforted Leila and she continued to service the two policies. Upon maturity, Leila was set to get partial payouts of KSh 69,000 yearly from the year 2018 to 2022 then in in 2023 she would get the total sum assured of KSh 455,450 for her son’s higher education.

“I was okay with this explanation, according to me, though separated, we had a common interest and I didn't imagine that someone could target his son's fee,” Leila said, adding that in 2008 when her son was ready to join high school, she went back to the insurance agency to check on the policy.

Account drained

At the insurance, she was required to bring over her husband to sign the handover of the policy to her because she had been the one paying.

“He did not resist the handing over and I even asked the lady agent to just do a cheque of the whole amount to the school, to which she agreed. But she had to call the head office to get started on the processing of the fund. The shock was on me! My ex-husband had already withdrawn the money two weeks back!” Leila said.

She was crushed. She did not know how to face her son who was now waiting for her at the reception area, and tell him that there was no school fee.

“I cried a river. I was told by the insurance that a policyholder has all rights to the policy and there was nothing they could do since the money had already been put in his account. Mr. Man was there, not moved at all. To him, there was nothing wrong with what he had done.” she added.

Leila had to think fast and find a way to ensure her son is admitted into the school.

However, for the second son’s cover, Leila was advised to go back to the office in June, just about time for the maturity of the second one.

“I purposed to focus on June like we had been told by the insurance hoping that at least second partial maturity and the other monies could be recovered.”

But another shock was awaiting. Her husband had gone back to the insurance agency in March 2019 and took a loan against the policy.

He had all the rights to the policy, he even removed Leila as “The payer” and as the “next of kin” and replaced her with his sister.

“Two weeks later I was called to the office to be informed about this and I felt so vulnerable. Why did the insurance allow him to take the loan yet this had been marked as a disputed policy? I asked all these questions but was told that the holder has all the rights to the policy. My insurance company let me down!” “That's how I lost my money which I had saved for 13 years and was meant for a very noble cause. Nilibaki tu na receipts. (I was only left with the receipts to show).”

“I will strive to give my totos (babies) the best, without bitterness and anger. Someday in their lives, they will have an adult conversation with their father. But for now, I will let them remain children, I will take all the blows for them, and guard their vulnerable hearts.”“One day it will get better.”
